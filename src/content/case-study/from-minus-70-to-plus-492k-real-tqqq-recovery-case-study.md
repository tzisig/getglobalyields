## Why This Case Study Matters

TQQQ fell approximately 79% in 2022 - from roughly $91 at its November 2021 peak to approximately $16.74 at its December 2022 low. Most investors who experienced that decline either sold at a loss or held passively and waited. This investor did neither.

What follows is a detailed account of how a non-US investor entered near the bottom of that drawdown, layered an options income strategy on top of the leveraged position, and built a portfolio currently worth approximately 3.5x the total capital invested - while navigating the specific complications of currency risk and tax treatment that non-US TQQQ investors face.

The numbers are real. The conditions that made them possible - a near-historic entry point followed by one of the strongest Nasdaq recoveries in history - are not guaranteed to repeat.

---

## Part 1: What Actually Went Wrong (January 2022 - Late 2022)

The starting capital of $100,000 was invested in January 2022, near TQQQ's all-time high of approximately $91 per share. TQQQ peaked at $91.68 on November 19, 2021 and began declining shortly after as the Federal Reserve signaled rate hikes.

**What the investor did:**
- Traded options aggressively without a systematic framework
- Increased position size during losses rather than reducing exposure
- Treated options as a directional instrument rather than an income tool
- Monitored positions constantly, which led to emotional decision-making

**What that caused:**
- Time decay (theta) worked against long option positions
- 3x leverage amplified every directional mistake
- Emotional decisions replaced the discipline the strategy required

**Result:** $100,000 → approximately $28,000 within twelve months.

TQQQ's 2022 calendar year return was -79.08%. An investor who bought at the January 2022 open and held passively would have lost approximately 72% of their capital by year-end. This investor's losses were similar - compounded by active options trading that added losses on top of the underlying decline.

The key price context: TQQQ closed at approximately $16.74 on December 28, 2022 - its lowest point. From TQQQ's November 2021 peak to that December 2022 low was a decline of approximately 81.7%.

---

## Part 2: The Turning Point (Late 2022)

At the bottom, the investor stopped trading and started thinking about the structural question: what is the base case for TQQQ from this level?

> **The thesis developed at the bottom:**
>
> The Nasdaq-100 had recovered from every major crash in its history - the dot-com collapse (2000-2002), the 2008 financial crisis, and the 2020 COVID crash. Each recovery eventually exceeded the prior peak.
>
> TQQQ at approximately $16-$17 represented a starting point where even a partial recovery would produce substantial returns at 3x leverage. The question was not whether the Nasdaq would recover, but whether the investor could hold through the recovery without being forced to sell.
>
> This was not certainty. It was a probability bet on the structure of the US technology sector - and it required eliminating the behaviors that caused the initial losses.

**What changed:** The investor stopped active options trading and began designing a systematic income structure instead of reactive speculation.

## Part 3: The Strategy Rebuild (February 2023 Onwards)

### The Core Position

In February 2023, the investor committed an additional $40,000 as cash collateral - bringing total invested capital to $140,000. Approximately 2,000 shares of TQQQ were purchased at approximately $25 per share.

**Why $25 was a defensible entry:**

TQQQ at $25 in early 2023 was still approximately 73% below its November 2021 peak of $91. The Nasdaq-100 had declined approximately 38% peak-to-trough in 2022 - its worst year since 2008. From a historical recovery standpoint, entering TQQQ at $25 after an index decline of that magnitude represented a starting point where the recovery math was favorable: QQQ would need to recover approximately 15-20% from its early 2023 level to bring TQQQ back above $40. QQQ was trading at approximately $275-$295 at the time of entry; recovering to $320-$340 - still well below its 2021 peak of $399 - would more than double the TQQQ position.

The $40,000 in cash collateral served a specific purpose: it allowed the investor to sell cash-secured puts without broker margin, reducing the leverage and risk profile of the short put positions.

---

### Layer 1: Covered Calls - Monthly Premium Income

The investor sold call options continuously against the 2,000 TQQQ shares.

**The practical rules:**
- After strong rallies: sell strikes further out of the money (higher strikes, less premium but more upside participation)
- In sideways or flat markets: sell strikes closer to at-the-money (more premium, more cap on upside)
- Roll when the position needed adjustment - not automatically, but based on the specific situation

**The premium mechanics:**

A covered call on TQQQ at a strike 5-10% above the current price typically generated monthly premium of approximately $1.50-$3.00 per share in 2023, when implied volatility (IV) was elevated. On 2,000 shares, that represented $3,000-$6,000 per month in gross premium income - approximately $36,000-$72,000 annualized against a position worth roughly $50,000 at entry.

As TQQQ's price rose, the premium per contract declined proportionally as a percentage of strike price, but rose in absolute terms due to the higher dollar value of each contract.

**The honest accounting:**
- Covered calls capped upside during multiple sharp rallies - most notably during TQQQ's +198% year in 2023
- Shares were called away twice during strong rallies, requiring re-entry at higher prices
- Despite these caps, the cumulative premium income was significant and contributed 30-40% of the total portfolio return over the period

---

### Layer 2: Short Puts - Additional Premium With Defined Entry Points

The investor sold put options using the $40,000 cash collateral position.

**The practical rules:**
- After drops: sell put strikes closer to the current price (higher premium, more downside risk)
- In calm markets: sell strikes further below (less premium, more cushion)

**The premium mechanics:**

A cash-secured put on TQQQ at a strike 10-15% below the current price typically generated premium of $1.00-$2.50 per contract per month. On the $40,000 in collateral - supporting roughly 15-20 contracts depending on the strike level - that represented $1,500-$5,000 per month in additional premium.

**The honest accounting:**
- Some puts were assigned at unfavorable timing - receiving shares when TQQQ continued declining after assignment
- Selling puts during falling markets increased total exposure, which required conviction to maintain
- One assignment cycle added approximately 500 shares at an effective cost basis below the market price, which later appreciated meaningfully

---

### The Combined Premium Picture

Over the January 2023 to April 2026 period, the combined covered call and short put premium income contributed approximately 30-40% of the total portfolio return - with the underlying TQQQ appreciation accounting for the remaining 60-70%.

The income was not linear: 2023 generated the highest option premium due to elevated implied volatility, while 2024-2025 saw lower per-contract premiums as volatility normalized. In a rough approximation, the strategy generated:

- 2023: approximately $4,000-$6,000/month in combined premium (high IV environment)
- 2024: approximately $2,500-$4,000/month as volatility fell and TQQQ price rose
- 2025-2026: approximately $3,000-$5,000/month as IV stabilized at moderate levels

These are approximate ranges, not audited figures. The key insight is that the premium income was sufficient to fund meaningful reinvestment while also partially hedging against short-term TQQQ price volatility.

---

## Part 4: What Actually Drove Returns

Not one thing. A combination:

| Driver | Contribution |
|---|---|
| TQQQ underlying recovery | 60-70% |
| Options premium income | 30-40% |

The 3x leverage of TQQQ is what made the recovery so dramatic - and what made 2022 so painful. A standard investment in QQQ would have recovered significantly, but would not have provided the same options premium engine. TQQQ's higher price and higher implied volatility produced substantially more premium income than the same dollar amount in QQQ.

## Part 5: The Currency Risk That Most USD Guides Ignore

The investor is a non-US investor whose functional expenses are in a local currency. The portfolio is denominated entirely in USD. This creates a second exposure that is independent of TQQQ's performance.

**The USD/ILS data:**

- January 2022 entry: approximately 3.22 ILS per USD
- December 2022 (TQQQ bottom): approximately 3.45 ILS per USD (USD had strengthened)
- April 2026 (current): approximately 3.10-3.18 ILS per USD

The USD depreciated approximately 11.46% against the ILS in 2026 alone (exchange-rates.org, 2026 data). Over the full period January 2022 to April 2026, the USD/ILS rate moved from approximately 3.22 to approximately 3.10-3.15 - a depreciation of roughly 2-4%.

**What this means in practice:**

The portfolio grew from $140,000 to approximately $492,000 in USD terms - a 251% gain on total capital. In ILS terms, applying the approximately 3% USD depreciation over the holding period, the real gain in local purchasing power is approximately 243% - meaningfully strong, but 8 percentage points less than the USD number suggests.

For 2026 specifically, where USD/ILS fell 11.46% YTD, a portfolio that gained 20% in USD terms would have gained only approximately 8.5% in ILS terms. This is the currency risk that matters most for non-US investors, and it operates independently of how well the investment strategy performs.

**The practical implication:** Non-US investors measuring TQQQ returns against local-currency obligations should always calculate the USD-to-local-currency return, not just the USD nominal return. In years when the USD weakens significantly against the investor's home currency, the effective return can be substantially lower than the headline figure.

## Part 6: What Did Not Go Smoothly

**Drawdowns during the recovery:**
The portfolio declined from approximately $465,000 to approximately $340,000 during one correction phase - a paper loss of $125,000. No hedge was in place. Exposure remained high. The discipline required to not exit during that decline was the primary psychological challenge of the strategy.

**Covered call assignment pain:**
In TQQQ's strongest rally phases - particularly in 2023 when TQQQ gained 198% - shares were called away multiple times at strike prices that looked attractive at the time of writing but were significantly below where TQQQ traded by expiration. Re-entering at higher prices cost real returns relative to a pure buy-and-hold on the same position.

**Put assignment complications:**
Selling puts in declining markets increased total TQQQ exposure at times when the investor was already underwater. One assignment cycle added shares when TQQQ was still falling, creating temporary paper losses on the newly acquired position before the recovery.

**The psychological reality:**
Managing a 3x leveraged position through a $125,000 paper loss drawdown requires a level of conviction that cannot be simulated in advance. This was less emotionally difficult than the 2022 decline - because the position was structured and had a defined income component - but still required active discipline not to exit.

## Part 7: Failure Scenarios

This strategy does not work in all market environments:

| Scenario | Why it breaks |
|---|---|
| Prolonged sideways market (2-5 years) | Volatility decay erodes TQQQ; option premiums may not fully offset losses |
| Repeated sequential downtrends | Put assignments stack positions at declining prices; capital locks into depreciated shares |
| Entry at wrong timing (e.g., TQQQ at $80+) | Deep drawdown with no income cushion large enough to compensate |
| Liquidity pressure | Multiple open positions reduce flexibility to adjust when conditions change |
| Sustained USD strength against home currency | Premium income and capital gains reduced in local currency terms |

The last scenario is particularly relevant for non-US investors. Had the USD strengthened 15% against the ILS over this period instead of weakening 3%, the local currency return would have been approximately 15 percentage points lower - illustrating that currency movement can dominate option income effects in extreme periods.

---

## Part 8: What Changed - Before and After

| Before | After |
|---|---|
| Constant position monitoring | Defined review schedule, fewer interventions |
| Emotional decisions based on short-term moves | Written rules for strike selection and rolling |
| Overtrading and adjusting positions reactively | Letting positions run to expiration when within parameters |
| 100% allocation to a single leveraged position | Options income as a systematic layer, cash collateral as buffer |

The transformation was behavioral, not technical. The strategy mechanics - selling covered calls and cash-secured puts - are widely understood. What changed was the discipline to follow a written framework instead of reacting to daily price movements.

### The Three Scenarios for January 2027

**Scenario 1: TQQQ above $62.50 at expiry → Portfolio approximately $620,000**
- Shares called away at $62.50: 6,000 × $62.50 = $375,000
- Both option positions expire worthless: $114,150 liability disappears, becoming retained premium
- Net gain from price move above current: 6,000 × ($62.50 - $58.59) = $23,460
- After expiry: immediately sell 120 new covered calls + 120 new short puts at approximately $10.00 each
- New premium locked in: approximately $240,000 against the next expiry cycle

**Scenario 2: TQQQ flat (~$58.59) at expiry → Portfolio approximately $597,000**
- Both options expire worthless
- $114,150 in option liability disappears, retained as income
- Portfolio value: 6,000 × $58.59 + $114,150 retained premium = approximately $597,000

**Scenario 3: TQQQ below $50 at expiry → Strategy continues**
- Puts assigned: receive 6,000 additional shares at effective cost basis approximately $50 - $10.10 premium = ~$39.90 per share
- Now holding 12,000 shares total at blended cost basis
- Sell 120 new puts at approximately $10.00 → $120,000 additional premium
- Wait for recovery to $50 or above → portfolio value above $720,000 if recovery occurs

**The only real risk:** A prolonged decline below $30 that persists for multiple years. This is a time problem, not a permanent loss problem - but time has a cost for investors with liquidity needs or finite holding horizons.

## Part 10: The Honest Bottom Line

This outcome - $140,000 growing to $492,000 in three-plus years - worked because three conditions aligned:

1. **Entry near a historic low.** Buying approximately 2,000 TQQQ shares at $25 in February 2023 came approximately 3 months after the December 2022 bottom of $16.74. The entry was good, but not perfectly timed.

2. **An exceptional recovery.** TQQQ returned 198% in 2023, 58% in 2024, and 34% in 2025. This is not a normal three-year return for any asset class. A more typical three-year Nasdaq recovery would have produced materially different results.

3. **The options income cushioned volatility.** The 30-40% contribution from options premium was not large enough to compensate for a permanent bear market, but it was large enough to provide income during sideways periods and to reduce the psychological pressure of holding through drawdowns.

> **The biggest risk is not volatility. It is applying this framework in the wrong market environment - specifically, entering with a large TQQQ position at elevated prices without the entry advantage that made this case study possible.**

This is not repeatable on demand. If the same entry conditions do not exist, results change completely.
